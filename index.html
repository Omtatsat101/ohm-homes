Real Estate Knowledge

What every buyer, seller, and investor should know.

📐

The 1% Rule

Monthly rent should be ≥1% of purchase price. $300K home → $3,000/mo. Below 0.8% is usually a bad cash flow deal unless banking on appreciation.

📊

Cap Rate 101

NOI ÷ Property Value. 6%+ is solid for residential rentals. Below 4% means you're paying for appreciation, not yield. Always calculate — don't just trust asking price.

½

The 50% Rule

Expect ~50% of gross rent to go to expenses (taxes, insurance, repairs, vacancy, mgmt) — not including mortgage. $2,000 rent → ~$1,000 true expenses.

❄️

Best Time to Buy

November–February has 10–15% less competition. Sellers who list in winter are often more motivated. Fewer buyers = more negotiating power.

🔍

Inspection Non-Negotiables

Foundation, roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, water damage. These are the expensive ones. Everything else is cosmetic. Never skip an inspection.

📈

Equity Builds Slowly at First

First 5 years of mortgage payments are 70%+ interest. Extra principal payments early have the highest impact. After year 15, the flip happens.

🏦

DTI Ratios

Lenders want front-end DTI (housing costs ÷ income) below 28% and back-end (all debt ÷ income) below 36–43%. Know your ratios before applying.

📉

House Hacking

Buy a 2-4 unit property, live in one unit, rent the others. Renters pay your mortgage. FHA allows 3.5% down on 2-4 units. One of the best starter strategies.

🔄

1031 Exchange

Sell an investment property and defer capital gains tax by rolling proceeds into a like-kind property. 45 days to identify, 180 days to close. Powerful wealth-building tool.

Frequently Asked Questions

Real answers to real estate questions — no jargon.

How accurate are online home value estimates?+
Online automated estimates (including our calculator) can vary ±10–20% from actual market value. They use historical sales data and appreciation trends, not a physical inspection or neighborhood context. For a precise value, use a licensed appraiser ($400–600) or ask a local agent for a free Comparative Market Analysis (CMA). Our tool is best for tracking appreciation trends over time, not for setting a listing price.
When does buying beat renting financially?+
Buying typically wins financially after 5–7 years in the same home, assuming normal appreciation. The break-even depends on: your price-to-rent ratio (buying wins below ~20x), how long you stay (shorter = renting wins), and your down payment opportunity cost. In high-cost cities (NYC, SF, LA), renting often beats buying unless you plan to stay 10+ years. Use the Rent vs Buy tab to run your specific scenario.
What is a good cash-on-cash return for a rental property?+
In 2025, 7–10% cash-on-cash is solid for most US markets. Below 5% and you're banking almost entirely on appreciation. The 1% rule (monthly rent ≥ 1% of purchase price) is a quick screen — properties that pass it usually generate positive cash flow. Use our Rental ROI tab to calculate your exact return including mortgage, expenses, and vacancy assumptions.
How much house can I actually afford?+
Lenders use two DTI ratios: front-end (housing costs ÷ gross income) below 28%, and back-end (all monthly debt ÷ income) below 36–43%. A practical rule: your mortgage payment (PITI) should be no more than 25–28% of take-home pay — not gross income. Use the Affordability tab with your real income and debts to get a personalized max purchase price. Always leave buffer — job loss, repairs, and life happen.
Should I put 20% down or invest the difference?+
20% down eliminates PMI (typically 0.5–1% of loan/year) and lowers your payment, but ties up capital. With mortgage rates at 6–7%, putting that extra money in the S&P 500 (historically ~10%/yr) could outperform PMI savings. The math depends on your rate, PMI cost, and investment returns. Most financial advisors suggest: put 10–20% down, keep 6 months emergency reserve, invest the rest.
What drives home appreciation over time?+
The biggest drivers are: (1) Local job and population growth — metros with strong employment attract buyers. (2) Supply constraints — coastal cities can't build fast enough. (3) Interest rates — lower rates increase purchasing power and push prices up. (4) Inflation — homes are real assets that track inflation over time. Nationally, homes have appreciated ~4–5%/year over 30 years, but individual markets vary wildly. Always research the specific submarket.
What is PMI and how do I avoid it?+
PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance) is required when your down payment is less than 20%. It protects the lender, not you — and costs 0.5–1% of the loan annually added to your payment. To avoid it: put 20% down, use a piggyback loan (80/10/10 structure), or ask about lender-paid PMI (higher rate but no separate PMI). Once your LTV drops below 80% through payments or appreciation, you can request PMI cancellation — it doesn't automatically drop off.
